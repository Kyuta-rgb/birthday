Kyulen — 20 years of becoming.

Childhood

Life, at the beginning, felt like a quiet morning that didn’t know storms existed yet.

I didn’t understand what loss meant when it first came taking my older sibling. At four years old, death didn’t have a definition in my mind — it just felt like something had been taken from a place I couldn’t name. It left behind a strange thought… like I needed someone older, someone stronger — a firstborn sister who could carry what I didn’t yet understand how to hold.

But even with that shadow, my childhood wasn’t empty. It had light.

Dalmus was that light. Where he was, I was there. We moved through days like two shadows that never separated — school, play, everything in between. Life felt simple when you didn’t question it.

There were others too. Fridah… a soft memory that never really left. Childhood love doesn’t arrive loudly — it just exists, quietly shaping how you learn to feel. Even now, there’s a part of me that wishes I could go back and tell her what she meant… how much of that innocence stayed with me longer than I realized.

Then life shifted.

My parents separated when I was in class four. And just like that, “home” stopped being a fixed place and became something temporary… something you moved with instead of returned to. The rentals became the background of my growing years — walls that watched more than they ever held.

Brenda became another constant. A friendship that filled spaces where things had started to break. She introduced me to explicit content at that small age. Traumatized me and left me wound.

But not everything that came into my life was gentle. Some things arrived too early — like being shown parts of the world I wasn’t ready to understand. They didn’t just pass through me… they stayed. They changed the way I saw people. The way I saw safety.

Got sexually abused by Jackline when I was that young still. Traumatized me had to retariate and had to start working on me. Became introvert at that age. I stopped trusting ladies and above all the world.

There are moments from that time that don’t feel like memories… they feel like fractures.

And when something breaks that early, you don’t always cry loudly. Sometimes, you just… go quiet.

I became quieter. More withdrawn. Not because I wanted to disappear — but because it felt safer to exist where nothing could reach me.

Trust didn’t leave all at once. It faded. Slowly. Especially toward the people I didn’t understand anymore.

And then there was the moment that almost ended everything before it even had time to begin. A game. A rock. A sudden impact. A broken skull. Near death experience.

The kind of silence that follows something serious… the kind that reminds you how thin the line between “here” and “gone” really is.

I didn’t fully understand it then. But I carried it.

Childhood, for me, was never just one thing. It was light and fracture. Laughter and silence. Connection and distance.

I saw things I shouldn’t have seen that early. Heard things that stayed longer than they should have. Watched life end… more than once.

And somehow, through all of that…

something didn’t collapse.

It hardened — not into something cold — but into something that could survive.

The trauma didn’t just break me. It built something in me that refused to disappear.

Early Teenage Years

Life didn’t slow down — it kept changing shape in ways I didn’t fully understand at the time.

School became mandatory, predictable, something I had to move through every day. But inside that routine, I was still trying to understand myself after everything my childhood had already left behind emotionally.

My mother’s care was my anchor. It felt like I was slowly healing in a space that still felt like home. For a while, I had something close to family I could hold onto. I had my mom. I had my small sister. I had moments that felt normal again.

But even in that “normal,” something inside me had already shifted.

I felt okay… but I was still lonely.

Somewhere along the way, I lost the ease of making friendships. Not because I didn’t meet people — I did — but because I stopped trusting connection the same way again.

In class six, something changed in my home life. I got a new father figure, and before I had fully learned how to adjust to his presence and behavior, there were experiences that affected me deeply. I don’t revisit them often, but I can say they pulled me back into myself. I became quieter again. More withdrawn. Not because I chose to, but because it felt safer that way.

That same period also brought something steady into my life — my cousin Morgan. He became more than family. He became a constant presence, someone who didn’t feel temporary. We grew up together from that point onward, and that bond stayed solid through everything.

Then came high school.

High school was strange for me — I met many people, but I never really met many friends. Except one.

Simon.

Even “best friend” doesn’t feel like a strong enough word for what that was. There should be something beyond best — something deeper, something that carries understanding without needing explanation.

We stood for each other in ways that shaped both of us. When I was down — through bullying, accusations, fights, and pressure — he stood with me. And when he faced his own struggles, I stood with him too. We didn’t just go through school together. We grew through it together.

That connection didn’t end when school ended. It stayed, quietly but firmly, beyond it.

My literature teacher was also one of the few consistent presences throughout high school — someone who saw me not just as a student, but as someone carrying more than what was visible on the surface.

And somewhere in the middle of all that, I experienced something I still remember softly.

In class eight, I met Maryanne — my first real feeling of love.

It wasn’t loud. It wasn’t complicated at first. It was simple in a way only childhood emotions can be — pure, unpolished, real.

She had a warmth in her presence that made everything around her feel lighter. She wasn’t just someone I liked — she was someone I felt. Someone who made me understand care in a different way. A quiet connection that didn’t need to be defined to be real.

Even now, that memory doesn’t feel like something that ended — it feels like something that simply passed through my life to shape how I understood love, attention, and presence.

I didn’t come to campus carrying just a dream.

I came carrying something quieter than that… but heavier.

A belief that I could become a software engineer. Not because it was easy, but because it felt like the only place my mind made sense.

But life didn’t agree with that direction.

My KCSE results shifted me into Public Health.

At first, I told myself it was fine. That life was just redirecting me. But deep down, something inside me knew — I was stepping into a life I had not fully chosen yet.


🎓 Campus — “Where people feel close, but not always safe”

Jomo Kenyatta University of Agriculture and Technology didn’t feel like a beginning.

It felt like being dropped into a place where everyone was already learning how to survive in their own way.

People were everywhere.

But trust… was rare.

That’s where I met Mike.

At first, he felt like comfort in a new place — someone to walk with, sit with, talk to, exist beside while everything felt unfamiliar.

We moved like friends do when they’re trying to belong somewhere.

Then he stole my phone.

And something in me just went quiet.

Not anger first.

Just that familiar reminder that closeness does not always mean safety.


💫 Puwa — “A connection that felt alive”

Then there was her.

Puwa.

Trouble Doll.

She didn’t enter my life loudly. She just… appeared in a way I still don’t know how to explain.

Black dress. Calm presence. No effort to be seen, yet impossible to ignore.

I remember thinking — there is something about her I already understand, even before I know her.

And somehow… she understood me too.

We didn’t grow slowly.

We clicked.

Like two people who had been looking for something they couldn’t name.

We became lovers, but more than that — we became safe space for each other.

We shared secrets that never left the room. We shared music that filled silence. We shared moments in Kibali House where life outside campus didn’t feel real.

That kind of love doesn’t feel like a story when you’re in it.

It feels like breathing.

And I loved her in a way that didn’t need explanation.


🧠 Ted — “The mind I keep returning to”

Then there is Ted.

Ted is not someone you just describe — you experience him.

He thinks differently. Speaks differently. Even his silence feels like it’s processing something deeper than the room he’s in.

Sometimes slow, sometimes unpredictable — but always himself.

I argue with him often, not to win, but to understand how his mind moves.

There’s something in him that forces you to think more than you speak.

He jokes in a dark way that somehow still makes sense. He sees patterns in things I overlook. And even when I don’t agree with him, I don’t ignore him.

Because people like Ted don’t fit into normal categories of friendship.

They stay in your mind even when they’re not around.


🧭 Adek — “The calm intelligence I admire”

Adek is different.

There is clarity in the way she thinks. Precision in the way she speaks. And a kind of intelligence that doesn’t try to prove itself.

She doesn’t just say things — she means them in a structured way that makes you reflect after she’s done talking.

There’s something I admire about that.

Not loud confidence.

But quiet certainty.

🧠 Love and loss — “What stayed with me”

Love in campus didn’t come as one thing.

It came in layers.

There was a kind of love that felt pure — the kind that makes life feel alive in ways you don’t question.

And there was another kind that left confusion behind. The kind that reshapes how you understand trust, attachment, and distance.

I don’t separate them anymore.

They both taught me something.

One showed me how beautiful connection can feel. The other showed me how fragile it is.


🧠 “What this stage really is”

This is not a finished chapter.

It is a living one.

I am still here.

Still learning people. Still learning myself. Still carrying dreams that didn’t follow the path I first imagined.

I wanted software engineering because it felt like the language my mind speaks.

Now I am here in Public Health, trying to understand what that means for me… while still holding onto the part of me that builds things in silence.

And maybe the truth is simple:

I am not who I thought I would become. But I am still becoming


“The beginning of building”

Somewhere inside all of this — between lectures, friendships, love, confusion, and learning myself — another version of me started forming quietly.

I started learning programming.

Not because everything made sense… but because it gave my mind somewhere to go when everything didn’t.

At first it was just curiosity. Lines of code. Small wins. Broken logic. Late nights of trying again after failing. But slowly, it stopped feeling like just learning.

It started feeling like creation.

Like I could actually build something from nothing.


Now, I don’t just learn it.

I live it.

I run Kyulinet Tech Solutions and Code Learners Hub.

And even though it’s still the beginning — I can feel it.

Not as noise. Not as hype.

But as direction.

A quiet sense that something is forming from all the chaos I’ve lived through.

Not everything in my life has been stable. Not everything has been easy. But this… this feels like the first time I am building something that builds me back.

It’s still early.

But it’s real.

And it’s mine.

The number 20 always felt far away… until it wasn’t.

Now I’m here.

And when I look back, I don’t see a straight path. I see fragments of moments that somehow built me.

Some were soft. Some were heavy. Some I still don’t fully know how to explain.

But all of them stayed.
